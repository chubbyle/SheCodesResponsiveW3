This is a page about Croissant

A **croissant** is a French pastry that is typically crescent-shaped, which is the origin of its French name¹. The first recipe for the croissant as it is known today emerged only as recently as 1906¹. The classic crescent-shaped French pastry is made from a yeast-based dough, which is rolled and layered to incorporate butter¹. Because of the cost of butter, many bakers use cheaper substitutes such as margarine¹. Most bakeries in France offer two types of croissants: croissant and croissant au beurre (“croissant with butter”)¹. The former tends to be more breadlike, whereas the latter is much richer and, predictably, more buttery¹.

Golden, buttery croissants should be crisp and flaky on the outside and soft and tender on the inside, with a texture that can be almost pulled apart¹. Croissants are often stuffed with different sweet or savory fillings¹. The classic sweet-filled croissants use almonds, marzipan, or chocolate, whereas savory croissants might be filled variously with cheese, spinach, or ham¹. Croissants are a common part of a continental breakfast in many European countries and served as dessert all around the world².
